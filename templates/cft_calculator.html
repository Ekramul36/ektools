One formula, whichever unit you start with

At its core, cubic feet is always Length × Width × Height — the only thing that changes based on your starting unit is a conversion step applied before or after that multiplication. Working in feet needs no conversion at all; inches needs a division by 1,728; meters needs a multiplication by roughly 35.31; and centimeters needs a division by roughly 28,317. This calculator handles that conversion automatically based on the unit you pick, so you never need to remember which factor applies where.

How to calculate cubic feet from any unit

 

From feet: multiply length × width × height directly — the result is already in cubic feet.

From inches: multiply the three measurements together to get cubic inches, then divide by 1,728 (the number of cubic inches in one cubic foot).

From yards: multiply the three measurements together to get cubic yards, then multiply by 27 (the number of cubic feet in one cubic yard).

From meters: multiply the three measurements together to get cubic meters, then multiply by roughly 35.3147.

From centimeters: multiply the three measurements together to get cubic centimeters, then divide by roughly 28,316.85.

Real-world examples

 

📦 Choosing between two storage bins

A rectangular bin measures 24 inches long, 18 inches wide, and 15 inches deep — is it bigger than a bin advertised as 2 ft × 1.3 ft × 1 ft?

Bin A (inches): 24 × 18 × 15 = 6,480 in³ ÷ 1,728 = 3.75 CFT

Bin B (feet): 2 × 1.3 × 1 = 2.6 CFT

Bin A holds noticeably more, even though its dimensions in inches look like smaller numbers at first glance — converting both to the same unit before comparing avoids that kind of mix-up.

🚚 Estimating moving truck space

A moving truck's cargo area measures 3 meters long, 1.8 meters wide, and 1.9 meters high. How much is that in cubic feet, for comparing against a packing list quoted in CFT?

3 × 1.8 × 1.9 = 10.26 m³ × 35.3147 = ~362.3 CFT

That figure can then be compared directly against a moving company's packed-volume estimate, which is often quoted in cubic feet rather than cubic meters.

Calculating irregular shapes

 

If the space you're measuring isn't a simple rectangular box — an L-shaped storage area, for instance — split it mentally into two or more regular rectangular sections, calculate the cubic feet of each section separately using this calculator, and add the results together for the total volume. This works because volume is additive: the total space is exactly the sum of its individual rectangular parts, regardless of how they're arranged.

CFT for timber and stone — the trade-specific case

In India's timber and natural stone trade, CFT is calculated slightly differently in practice: length and width are measured in feet, but thickness is measured in inches, since it's a much smaller dimension. The formula is Length (ft) × Width (ft) × Thickness (in) ÷ 12. Switch to "Timber/Stone (ft + inch)" mode at the top of the calculator above to enter measurements exactly this way, without converting thickness to feet yourself first.

Frequently asked questions

 

How do I calculate cubic feet?

Measure the length, width and height of the item or space in feet, then multiply the three numbers together: Length × Width × Height = Cubic Feet.

How do I calculate cubic feet from inches?

Multiply length, width and height in inches together, then divide the result by 1,728 (since there are 1,728 cubic inches in one cubic foot).

How do I calculate cubic feet from meters or centimeters?

Multiply length, width and height in meters together, then multiply by 35.3147 to convert cubic meters to cubic feet. For centimeters, multiply the three measurements together and divide by 28,316.85.

What is CFT used for in the timber and stone trade?

CFT (Cubic Feet) is the standard unit for pricing timber, wood, marble and granite in India, typically measured as length and width in feet with thickness in inches.

Is this tool free?

Yes, completely free with no signup required.

Can I calculate an irregular shape with this tool?

Split the shape into regular rectangular sections, calculate each one separately, and add the results together for the total cubic feet.

What's the difference between cubic feet and square feet?

Square feet measures a flat area (length × width only); cubic feet measures volume, adding a third dimension — height or depth — to the calculation.

Does this account for the wall thickness of a container?

No, enter the internal (usable) dimensions of a box or container directly for an accurate result, since outer dimensions include material thickness that isn't available for storage.

Why do I need cubic yards as well as cubic feet?

Certain materials like concrete, mulch, and soil are commonly sold by the cubic yard rather than cubic foot, so having both figures available saves a separate conversion step.

Can I use decimal values for my measurements?

Yes, all fields accept decimal values like 2.5 or 10.75 for precise measurements.

Is there a separate mode for timber and stone trade calculations?

Yes, switch to "Timber/Stone (ft + inch)" mode at the top of the calculator to enter length and width in feet with thickness in inches directly, matching how the trade measures these materials.
