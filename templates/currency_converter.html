Exchange rates move constantly — a converter has to keep up

Unlike a length or weight conversion, which is a fixed mathematical relationship that never changes, a currency conversion depends on a rate that shifts continuously based on global trading activity — supply and demand for each currency, interest rate decisions, economic data releases, and broader market sentiment all push rates up or down, sometimes within the same trading day. A converter using a stale, hardcoded table can be noticeably wrong within days, which matters more than it might seem for anyone actually transacting, budgeting, or comparing prices across currencies rather than just doing a rough mental estimate.

Understanding the mid-market rate vs what you actually pay

 

The number this tool shows is the mid-market rate — the midpoint between the buy and sell prices being traded on global currency markets at that moment, essentially the "true" exchange rate before anyone adds a fee. This is the same reference rate you'd see quoted in financial news or on a currency chart, and it's the fairest baseline for understanding what an amount in one currency is genuinely worth in another.

Banks, money-changers, and international transfer services almost never actually give you the mid-market rate directly — they build in a margin, sometimes disclosed as a fee and sometimes simply baked invisibly into a slightly worse exchange rate than the market rate, which is how many "no commission" currency exchanges still make money. Knowing the true mid-market rate before a transaction — which is exactly what this tool provides — is what lets you spot how large that markup actually is and compare offers from different providers on equal footing, rather than trusting whatever rate is quoted at the counter.

This is expected and not an error — this tool shows the mid-market rate, while banks and transfer services add their own margin on top. The gap between the two is effectively the provider's fee, even when it isn't labeled as one separately.

The rate looks different from what I saw an hour ago. That's expected — currency markets move continuously throughout the day, so a rate checked in the morning and again in the evening can genuinely differ, sometimes by a meaningful amount during volatile periods.

Currency conversion for an Indian audience

 

India is one of the largest recipients of international remittances in the world, and for many households, an accurate sense of the USD-INR, AED-INR, or GBP-INR rate isn't academic — it directly affects how much a transfer from a family member abroad is actually worth once it lands. The rupee, like every currency, floats against others based on trade balances, interest rate differentials, and capital flows, which is why it's worth checking the current rate rather than relying on a number from even a few weeks earlier, especially during periods of higher volatility.

For anyone comparing remittance or money-transfer services, the practical approach is to check this tool's mid-market rate first, then compare it against the rate each individual service actually quotes for the same transfer — the difference between the two is the service's real margin, even when they advertise "zero fees," since the markup is often built into a worse exchange rate rather than a separate line-item charge.

Frequently asked questions

 

Are the exchange rates live?

Yes. Rates are fetched from a live exchange rate API and refreshed each time the page loads, reflecting current market rates rather than a fixed table.

How many currencies are supported?

150+ currencies including INR, USD, EUR, GBP, AED, BDT, JPY, and more, covering most currencies used in everyday international transactions.

Why do exchange rates change throughout the day?

Currency values float against each other continuously based on global trading activity, economic data, and market sentiment, so the rate at 9am and 5pm the same day can genuinely differ.

Will the converted amount match what my bank charges?

Not exactly. This shows the live market mid-rate; banks and money changers add their own margin and fees on top, so the amount you actually receive will usually be somewhat less favorable.

Is this tool free to use?

What happens if the live rate feed is unavailable?

The tool falls back to a set of approximate rates so conversions can still be estimated, with a note that live rates couldn't be loaded.
