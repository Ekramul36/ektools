Every text box has its own font selector, with classic meme fonts like Impact and Anton, plus more playful options like Bangers and Permanent Marker for a hand-drawn look. You can also adjust the font size with a slider, so a short punchline can go big and bold while a longer caption stays readable.

4. Customize text and outline color

Click the color swatches next to any text box to change both the fill color and the outline color independently. White text with a black outline is the classic look, but if your background is light, flipping to dark text with a white outline will read much better. Testing both combinations against your specific photo takes only a few seconds and often makes the difference between text that pops and text that blends awkwardly into the image.

Why the outline matters: a bold outline keeps text readable regardless of what's directly behind it in the photo. Plain colored text without an outline can disappear against a busy or similarly-toned background — the outline is doing more work than it looks like at first glance.

5. Drop in stickers

Click the "Stickers" button to open a palette of emoji-style stickers — fire, laughing faces, thumbs up, sunglasses, and more. Tap one to add it to your meme, then drag it into place and resize it with the slider. It's a quick way to add extra punch without needing any design skill, and works well combined with text boxes for reaction-style memes where a sticker reinforces the caption's tone rather than repeating it.

6. Apply a filter

If the photo itself needs a mood shift, the Filters row lets you switch between Grayscale, Sepia, Bright, Dark, and Invert with one click. This applies to the image only, so your text and stickers stay sharp and readable on top regardless of which filter is active, and switching filters is instant so it's easy to try a few before settling on one.

Popular meme formats you can recreate

Multi-panel comparison

Two or four images side by side, each with its own short caption — built by uploading a pre-combined grid image, then adding a text box per panel.

Speech-bubble callout

A short text box positioned near a subject's face, sized small and placed precisely using drag-and-drop rather than a fixed top/bottom slot.

Classic top/bottom

The original image-macro format — one text box locked near the top, another near the bottom, both in Impact for maximum readability.

Because text boxes aren't restricted to fixed positions, all three of these formats — and any variation on them — work with the same editor rather than needing a different template for each style. That flexibility is the main practical advantage over meme generators that only support one rigid layout: instead of picking a template that almost fits your idea, you build the exact layout the joke needs.

A short history of meme formats

The "top text, bottom text" layout that most people associate with memes traces back to image macros from early internet forums, where Impact font in all caps became the de facto standard because it was bold enough to read at small thumbnail sizes and worked on nearly any background. That legacy is why so many meme tools still default to a fixed two-line layout, even though modern meme culture has moved well past it — reaction images, multi-panel formats, and speech-bubble captions are all common now, which is exactly why a tool with unlimited, freely draggable text boxes fits how memes are actually made today rather than how they were made fifteen years ago.

Understanding this history also explains a design choice worth keeping in mind: the classic bold-outline, all-caps style isn't just an aesthetic preference — it's a legibility solution that emerged before high-resolution displays and fast image compression were the norm, when memes often got re-shared and re-compressed multiple times before reaching a viewer. A bold outline survives that repeated compression far better than thin, unoutlined text, which is part of why the look has stuck around even now that compression quality is rarely a real constraint.

Tips for a meme that actually lands

  • Keep captions short — a meme is read in under two seconds by most viewers; a caption that requires re-reading usually isn't landing as intended.
  • Match the font to the tone — Impact reads as classic/ironic, Bangers and Permanent Marker read as more playful or sincere; picking the wrong one can undercut the joke.
  • Don't overcrowd with stickers — one or two well-placed stickers add punch; five or six can make the image feel cluttered and slow down the read.
  • Check readability on mobile — most memes are viewed on a phone screen at a small size, so text that looks fine full-size on a desktop editor can become hard to read once shrunk down; preview at a smaller size before finalizing.
  • Save a version without stickers/filters if unsure — a plain, clean version is easy to fall back on if a heavily edited version doesn't land the way you expected.
  • Crop before adding text — if the source photo has distracting background elements, cropping first gives the text boxes a cleaner area to sit on, rather than fighting a busy backdrop with a heavier outline.
  • Reuse a strong template — if a particular layout works well for your group or audience, keeping the same font and color choices across future memes builds a recognizable, consistent style rather than starting from scratch each time.
