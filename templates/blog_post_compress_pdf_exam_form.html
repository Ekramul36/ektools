How PDF compression actually reduces file size

Two different techniques usually run together inside a PDF compressor, and knowing the difference helps set the right expectations:

Lossless optimization

Removes redundant internal data — duplicate fonts, unused metadata, inefficient encoding — without touching how the page actually looks. This alone often cuts a text-heavy PDF significantly.

Image re-compression

Embedded images (like a scanned photo or signature) are re-encoded at a lower quality setting, which is where most of the size reduction comes from on scan-heavy PDFs.

A PDF that's mostly typed text with no images can shrink dramatically through lossless optimization alone, sometimes reaching under 100 KB from a much larger starting file without any visible change. A PDF that's essentially a photographed page, on the other hand, depends much more heavily on image re-compression, which is why scanned documents sometimes need the JPG-first workaround described below to hit an aggressive target.

A quick pre-upload checklist

Before submitting a compressed PDF to any exam or job portal, running through a short checklist catches most last-minute rejections:

  • Confirm the file size in your file manager, not just by eye — a file that "looks small" can still be a few KB over a strict limit.
  • Open the downloaded PDF and check every page loaded correctly — a compression step that partially fails can occasionally drop content, so a quick visual check before submitting is worth the extra minute.
  • Re-read the exact wording of the size requirement — some notifications specify "up to 100 KB" (inclusive) while others say "less than 100 KB" (exclusive), and submitting exactly at the boundary can go either way depending on interpretation.
  • Check the accepted file format — most portals want PDF specifically for documents and JPG for photos; submitting the wrong format type, even at the right size, causes a rejection unrelated to compression at all.

Why exam forms demand such tiny PDF sizes

Most government exam portals in India were built years ago on servers with strict upload limits, often 100KB to 1MB per file. During peak form-submission periods with lakhs of applicants uploading documents simultaneously, these limits keep the servers from crashing under the combined load. The result: your scanned certificate, mark sheet, or signed document — which might easily be 2-5 MB straight out of a phone camera — needs to be squeezed down dramatically before the form will accept it.

This isn't a quirk of any one board — nearly every major recruitment portal in India (SSC, Railway RRB, IBPS, UPSC, and most state PSCs) enforces some version of this limit, which is why compressing PDFs before a form submission has become a routine step for anyone applying to competitive exams.

Tips to get the smallest file size without losing quality

  • Scan documents in black-and-white or grayscale mode instead of color when the content allows it — color scans carry far more data per page than grayscale ones.
  • Avoid scanning at very high DPI (300 DPI is usually more than enough for text documents; anything higher mostly adds file size without adding legibility).
  • If your PDF is mostly one scanned image, consider converting it to JPG first and using EkZapp's Image Size Reducer to hit an exact KB target, then convert back to PDF.
  • Remove any blank or duplicate pages before compressing — every extra page adds to the total, even a mostly-empty one.
  • Flatten any unnecessary form fields or annotations layered on the PDF, since these add overhead separate from the visible page content and don't always get stripped by a general compression pass.
  • If multiple documents need to go into one submission, merge them first and then compress the combined file once, rather than compressing each separately — this avoids compounding quality loss across repeated passes.

Scanned PDFs vs. text-based PDFs

Not all PDFs compress the same way, and understanding why helps set realistic expectations:

Text-based PDF

Created digitally (e.g. exported from Word), the text itself takes almost no space. Compression mainly targets any embedded images, so these files often shrink dramatically.

Scanned PDF

Essentially one large image per page. There's less room to shrink without a visible quality trade-off, which is why very small targets sometimes need the JPG-first workaround above.
