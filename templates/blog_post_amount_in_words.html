How to Write Amount in Words for Cheques & GST Invoices

Published July 3, 2026 · 3 min read

Getting the amount-in-words line wrong on a cheque is one of the most common reasons banks reject payments. Here's the correct format to use — and a free tool that does it instantly, in the Indian Lakh/Crore system.

Why the amount in words matters

Every cheque and most formal invoices require the amount to appear twice: once in figures, once spelled out in words. This isn't just a formality — it's a fraud-prevention measure. Numeric figures can be altered with a stray pen stroke (turning a 1 into a 4, for example), but words are far harder to tamper with. If the figures and words don't match, banks are required to reject the cheque, so getting this line exactly right matters.

The correct cheque format

Amount in figures: ₹1,25,430.50
Amount in words: One Lakh Twenty-Five Thousand Four Hundred Thirty Rupees and Fifty Paise Only

Notice three things about this format:

  • It uses the Indian numbering system (Lakh, Crore) rather than the International system (Million, Billion).
  • Paise are spelled out separately after "and."
  • The word "Only" appears at the end — this closes the line so nothing can be added afterward.

Indian vs. International number format

ValueIndian FormatInternational Format
1,00,000One LakhOne Hundred Thousand
10,00,000Ten LakhOne Million
1,00,00,000One CroreTen Million

Indian banks and most GST invoices expect the Lakh/Crore format. If you're dealing with international clients, you may need the Million/Billion format instead.

Using amount in words on GST invoices

While not strictly mandatory under GST rules, spelling out the total amount in words on an invoice is standard business practice in India. It reduces disputes, helps with manual verification during audits, and looks more professional to clients — especially for high-value invoices.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do cheques need the amount written in words?

Banks require the amount in both figures and words as a fraud-prevention measure. If the numeric amount is altered, the written words act as the legally binding cross-check, so any mismatch causes the cheque to be rejected.

What is the difference between Indian and International number format?

Indian format groups numbers using Lakh and Crore (1,00,000 = One Lakh), while International format uses Thousand, Million, and Billion. Indian banks and GST invoices typically use the Lakh/Crore format.

Should I add "Only" at the end of a cheque amount?

Yes. Adding "Only" after the amount in words is standard banking practice — it closes the line and prevents anyone from adding extra words to alter the amount.

Do GST invoices legally require the amount in words?

Not always mandatory under GST rules, but including the total in words is considered best practice for clarity and is commonly expected by clients and auditors, especially for larger transactions.
