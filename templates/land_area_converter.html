Why It Matters

Why Bigha and Katha Aren't the Same Everywhere

Bigha is one of the oldest land measurement units in the Indian subcontinent, used long before the square foot or hectare became standard. Unlike metric units, which were defined once and applied uniformly, Bigha was fixed independently by regional rulers, zamindari records, and later British-era land administration in each province. That's why the same word "Bigha" can mean 14,400 sq ft in West Bengal and roughly 27,220 sq ft in Bihar — nearly double.

This becomes a real problem during land transactions. A buyer comparing a "2 Bigha plot in Bihar" against a "2 Bigha plot in West Bengal" without converting to a common unit could end up comparing a much larger parcel to a much smaller one.

Even within a state, Bigha isn't always one fixed number

Several states use two distinct sizes rather than a single value. Rajasthan distinguishes between a larger "Pucca" Bigha (about 27,225 sq ft) and a smaller "Kaccha" Bigha (about 17,424 sq ft), and Uttar Pradesh's Bigha shrinks noticeably moving from the eastern Purvanchal region toward the western districts. This calculator's state dropdown includes these named variants where they're well documented, though even finer district-level differences can still exist within a single state or Bigha type — always confirm the exact locally accepted figure before a transaction.

When visiting a plot in person, the dimensions marked on stakes or a rough boundary measurement are usually in feet, not Katha. Budgeting a Land Purchase by Katha, Bigha, or Sq Ft Rate

Land prices in eastern India are often quoted per Katha or per Bigha rather than per square foot, which makes it hard to compare a rural plot's asking price against a per-square-foot rate you might see for urban property. Comparing multiple plots fairly

If one plot is quoted at ₹5,00,000 per Katha and another at ₹150 per square foot, converting both to the same per-square-foot basis is the only reliable way to tell which is actually the better rate — glancing at the two headline numbers side by side is misleading since they're not on the same unit.

What this calculator doesn't include

The total cost shown here is purely area × rate. It doesn't account for registration charges, stamp duty, brokerage, or any premium for road access, shape, or corner-plot status — all of which typically add to the final amount you'll actually pay for a real transaction.

Buying Tips

Common Mistakes When Buying Land in Bigha or Katha

  • Assuming Bigha means the same thing everywhere — the single biggest source of confusion when comparing plots advertised in different states or even different districts of the same state.
  • Not verifying with local land records — the conversion factor a broker quotes verbally may not match the officially recognized figure in that district's revenue records, which is what ultimately matters for registration.
  • Comparing per-unit price without converting to a common basis — a lower price "per Katha" can still be a worse deal once converted to price per square foot, if the seller's Katha definition is smaller than you assumed.
  • Ignoring RERA requirements — for residential real estate transactions covered by RERA, area must legally be stated in square feet or square meters, so a traditional-unit-only listing for such property is itself a red flag worth investigating.
  • Skipping a physical measurement — relying purely on the paperwork's stated area without confirming actual boundary dimensions on site can miss encroachments or boundary disputes that shrink the usable area.
District Variation

When District-Level Bigha Values Differ Further

Beyond the state-to-state differences this converter handles, a handful of states have well-documented sub-variants that go a level deeper than the state alone. Rajasthan formally distinguishes between a larger "Pucca" Bigha and a smaller "Kaccha" Bigha, both used in different contexts within the same state, and Uttar Pradesh's Bigha shrinks considerably moving from the eastern Purvanchal belt toward western districts near Delhi. This converter includes these named variants as separate options in the state dropdown.

Beyond these documented cases, some districts apply further local adjustments to historical land records that aren't consistently published in a single authoritative source — which is exactly why every land conversion tool, including this one, comes with the same caution: treat the figure as a starting estimate, and confirm the exact locally accepted value with your district's land records office or a registered surveyor before it goes into any legal document.

Regional Focus

Bigha and Katha in Bengal, Bihar, UP and Assam

These four states account for the overwhelming majority of everyday Bigha and Katha usage in India, and each has its own conventions worth knowing before a transaction.

West Bengal

The most standardized of the four, with 1 Bigha fixed at 14,400 sq ft and 1 Katha at 720 sq ft across most of the state, making conversions here more reliable than in states with wider district variation.

Bihar

Bigha in Bihar runs noticeably larger than in Bengal, at roughly 27,220 sq ft, with 1 Katha around 1,361 sq ft — a difference significant enough that treating a Bihar Bigha and a West Bengal Bigha as interchangeable would overstate a Bengal plot's size by nearly half.

Uttar Pradesh

The state with the widest internal variation among the four — Purvanchal (eastern UP) uses a Bigha close to Bihar's at roughly 27,225 sq ft, while western UP districts use a considerably smaller figure, making it essential to know which part of the state a plot is in before applying any single conversion factor.

Assam

Assam's Bigha matches West Bengal's at 14,400 sq ft, but its Katha is defined differently — 2,880 sq ft in standard usage (5 Katha per Bigha) rather than Bengal's 720 sq ft (20 Katha per Bigha), so the two states' Bigha figures align while their Katha figures do not.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How many square feet are in 1 Bigha?

It depends on the state. 1 Bigha is 14,400 sq ft in West Bengal and Assam, about 27,220 sq ft in Bihar, and about 27,225 sq ft in Uttar Pradesh (Purvanchal) and Rajasthan (Pucca). Always confirm the exact local value before relying on it for a transaction.

How many square feet are in 1 Katha?

1 Katha is 720 sq ft in West Bengal, 2,880 sq ft in standard Assam usage, and roughly 1,361 sq ft in Bihar and Uttar Pradesh, since 1 Bigha equals 20 Katha in most of these regions.

Why does Bigha size differ between states?

Bigha is a pre-metric traditional unit standardized differently under regional and British-era administrations, so unlike the square foot or hectare, it was never unified nationally.

How many Bigha are in 1 Acre?

1 Acre equals 43,560 sq ft. In West Bengal that's roughly 3.03 Bigha, while in Bihar or Uttar Pradesh it's closer to 1.6 Bigha, since their Bigha is a larger unit.

How many Katha are in 1 Acre?

In West Bengal, where 1 Katha is 720 sq ft, 1 Acre works out to about 60.5 Katha. In Bihar, where 1 Katha is larger at about 1,361 sq ft, 1 Acre is closer to 32 Katha.

1200 square feet is how many Katha?

Using the West Bengal standard of 720 sq ft per Katha, 1,200 sq ft is about 1.67 Katha. In Bihar, where 1 Katha is about 1,361 sq ft, the same 1,200 sq ft works out to roughly 0.88 Katha.

How much built-up area fits on 1 Katha?

1 Katha (720 sq ft in West Bengal) is enough for a small house footprint, though the actual buildable area depends on local setback rules, floor area ratio (FAR), and how many floors the local municipal bylaws permit — this converter gives plot size only, not construction feasibility.

Is this converter accurate enough for legal documents?

This tool uses commonly referenced regional values for quick estimates. For registry, sale deeds, or legal filings, confirm the exact local conversion factor — including any district-level variation — with your state's land records department or a registered surveyor.

What is a Guntha, Kanal, or Marla?

Guntha (1,089 sq ft) is common in Maharashtra and Karnataka, Kanal (5,445 sq ft) and Marla (272.25 sq ft) are used in Punjab, Haryana, and Himachal Pradesh, and Cent (435.6 sq ft) is widely used in Kerala and Tamil Nadu.

What is Shotok? Is it the same as Decimal or Cent?

Yes. Shotok (শতক) is the Bengali and Bangladeshi name for Decimal — both refer to 1/100th of an Acre (435.6 sq ft), the same unit called "Cent" in Kerala and Tamil Nadu.
